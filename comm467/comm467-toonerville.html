Dek headline 40–65 characters

The Pennsylvania Department of Transportation is awarding 00 million in grants and low-interest loans to municipalities to upgrade and repair roads and bridges, with Toonerville receiving million for critical infrastructure projects, officials announced Thursday.

Mayor Patrice Henderson said the funding will help shore up the deteriorating Johnstone Bridge, which connects Toonerville to the Town of Gardiner over Beaver Creek. The 120-year-old span will close for 10 months beginning May 1 for extensive repairs.

"This plan gives us the money we need to get the job done as fast as possible and with minimal inconvenience to our citizens," Henderson said.

A 2022 report found significant cracks in the bridge's foundation and urged its immediate closure.

Freddie Smith, an activist who lives on Acorn Avenue, has been demonstrating near the bridge for weeks, carrying a sign saying, "Beware: Broken bridge!"

The grant will also fund a repaving of Route 112, a pothole-ridden corridor, and new streetlights along First Avenue, Toonerville's shopping district.

Statewide, the funding will extend beyond Toonerville. Portions of Interstate 80 that currently lack lighting will get new streetlights.

"It's about time," said David Joachim, a Penn State journalism professor who divides his time between State College and New York. "Have you ever made that trip at night, in the rain and fog?"

"This infrastructure money will help keep all Pennsylvanians safe on our roads, bridges and tunnels," Gov. Josh Shapiro said. "I'm proud of this bipartisan action."

Story Two — High School Sports

At just 16 years old, Robbie Courtier is already making his mark on the court — and off it. The 6-foot-7 sophomore center has powered the Toonerville Titans to a perfect 12-0 record, the team's best start in 20 years. Courtier leads the league with an average of 23 points per game, but he says the wins matter more.

"It's all about winning games," Courtier said. "Never how many points I score. The point is to win, always."

Basketball runs in the family. His mother, Jenna Courtier, was a member of Team USA for the 2000 Summer Olympics, though an injury kept her from playing.

His father, Stephan, who grew up in Queens, New York, dreams of seeing Robbie suit up for his hometown team.

"My dad was there for the heyday of St. John's basketball," Robbie said. "I know he'd be over the moon if I played there."

While he dreams of suiting up for a powerhouse program like Duke or St. John's, Robbie remains grounded. On weekends, he volunteers at the local soup kitchen.

"I was taught to give back," he said.

His coach, Billy Ray Peterson, calls him a rare talent with an even rarer attitude. "He's focused, he's disciplined, but most of all, he's kind-hearted and a leader by example," Peterson said. "This kid is a phenom on the court, yes, but he's also a really good guy."

For now, Courtier and the Titans have their sights set on maintaining their undefeated streak. His father keeps things in perspective.

"Look, I love that he's so talented and so motivated," Stephan Courtier said. "But I've always told him, your health and your happiness come first. Basketball comes later."
