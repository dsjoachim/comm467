Story One — Arson
Main Headline 32–40 characters
— ✦ —
Dek Headline 40–50 characters

A historic Heartland landmark is in shambles after a Saturday morning fire that police say was caused by arson. The Heartland Steakhouse, at 1701 Frederick Ave., suffered an estimated $125,000 in damages, mostly to the restaurant's main dining room.

Police arrested two men — Jake O'Neal, 23, and Arnold Simpson, 19 — on suspicion of arson. The pair was taken into custody six blocks from the restaurant at around 1 a.m. Saturday. Officers stopped the men on Faraon Street after observing them in a dark SUV — with a gasoline can and other items inside — that had been seen in the neighborhood just before the fire. Police said the men also smelled of gasoline.

Owner Bill McNee said the restaurant would be closed indefinitely. "I don't understand why anyone would set my restaurant on fire. I'm just totally baffled," McNee said.

McNee's father, John McNee, started the restaurant with his brother Jimmy in 1950, according to the restaurant's website. John McNee died of Covid-19 in 2020.

A separate fire two weeks ago burned down the Olive Garden restaurant in downtown Heartland. That fire remains under investigation, police said.

Story Two — City Planning
Main Headline 32–40 characters
— ✦ —
Dek Headline 40–50 characters

Where the sidewalk ends, debate begins. Heartland lacks sidewalks on parts of many of its major streets — most visibly on Missouri Avenue and Frederick Boulevard. In recent years, developers have been required to install sidewalks when making substantial changes to a property. But the city still has an ever-expanding, not-quite-connected patchwork.

Some builders and property owners have complained about being forced to build "sidewalks to nowhere," arguing it is wasteful to require a sidewalk on one parcel without addressing adjoining properties.

"We are quite concerned about the random placement of sidewalks," city architect Ron Matthis told City Council members Thursday. He suggested the city devote money to connecting long stretches — similar to funding for hiking and biking trails. "Aren't pedestrians as important as cyclists?" he asked.

Mayor Hakim Davis, who won election last November on promises to improve walkability and pedestrian safety, said the status quo is unsustainable. "We can't be serious about attracting young families and a new tax base if we don't improve our infrastructure," he said. "Sidewalks bring customers."

A city-commissioned study found that connecting all of Heartland's sidewalks would cost approximately $21 million. Davis said that cannot be raised through taxes alone and called on the Chamber of Commerce to persuade businesses that sidewalks are a worthwhile investment.

Phil Amos, owner of Heartland Auto Parts, was blunt about the idea of businesses footing the bill. "No way," he said.
